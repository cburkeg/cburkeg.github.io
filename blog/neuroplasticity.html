Neuroplasticity

Discuss how understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefits people

Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to change, reorganise, and rewire itself. It was previously thought that a person's brain was only neuroplastic over a specific period in childhood, but more recent research has shown that the brain remains neuroplastic even throughout adulthood. Evidence suggests that once students are told that their brains (and by extension their skills and abilities) are malleable and can be changed - rather than being static and fixed - those students have a more positive attitude towards learning and challenges. They are less deterministic and are willing to embrace difficulty, seeing it as an opportunity to improve their skills rather than a test of some intrinsic part or reflection of their person or their value. Knowing that the brain is malleable and that skills can be improved leads to better academic and career results overall.

Growth mindset

Discuss what it a growth mindset is and why it is relevant

I understand that a growth mindset is a belief that abilities and aptitudes can be developed and can improve over time with training. Having a growth mindset means that challenges are seen as opportunities to improve and develop skills further, and current abilities are recognised as non-fixed and changeable. This outlook similarly means that someone's performance at some task or ability is not necessarily reflective of their value or personal worth - it is instead just a snapshot of their ability in time, separate from their person. In other words, having a growth mindset allows you to positively receive constructive criticisim and feedback so that you can improve, rather than defensively react to what is incorrectly perceived as an attack on 'you'.

Link to a resource that you found particularly useful or engaging

I was interested to read some reflections on growth mindset by the author (Carol Dwerck) of the original paper popularising the growth mindset, written some time after her initial work. She emphasies that people, in reality, are a mixture of both fixed mindsets and growth mindsets. The author notes that it is easy to think that we can adopt a growth mindset once the benefits are shown to us, and it is easy to understand the logic of a growth mindset on an intellectual level, but it is harder to actually put into practice. We must watch ourselves internally to see how we actually respond to situations and challenges, and to note when we might be responding from a fixed mindset perspective (which, by the bye, also ties in with mindfulness.)

For example, when we are facing challenges, we should look inwardly and ask ourselves if any discomfort or anxiety arises. We can also look at others who are better than us at things we think are important - do we feel jealousy (as per a fixed mindset), or do we feel inspired (as per a growth mindset)? Dweck notes that we should be realistic and honest with ourselves about ourselves, or there is a danger of falling into 'false growth mindset'.

The article also discusses the potential danger of growth mindsets being used to make students feel better without actually improving their academic performance - in other words, instead of closing actual achievement gaps, there is a danger of hiding achievement gaps by reassuring students that everyone is equal. I think this is a good reminder that we need to use these tools at our disposal to actually achieve results and learn material, rather than simply feeling good about ourselves (which, of course, is important too).
