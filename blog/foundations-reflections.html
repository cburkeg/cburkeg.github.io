What are three new things you have learnt about yourself and your ego due to the core learning?

I don't think I've learned anything about myself that was entirely and categorically new, but I have got to know certain aspects of myself better, particularly through mindfulness. In particular, I reminded myself that I can struggle to work very productively when at home; although I can have productive days when the setting is right, a lot of the time a change of scene to get out of the house is very important. I have also found that it is still difficult to force myself to spend very long writing these blog posts and other parts of the core work (particularly writing reflections). I think this is because I still have some residual burn-out from my previous work. However, I'm etting better at managing this. Mindfulness is helpful to notice when my aversion to this work does arise, and to coach myself through to the other side.

What are the role of values, empathy, and self-awareness in learning and programming?

I'm not really sure if I can fully answer this honestly, because I feel like a lot of the core work has been quite separate from programming. What I would say is that although the coding we've done to date has been isolated and solo, working as a programmer is ultimately a job spent working with other people and is a human activity. We will need to work alongside teammates, so we need to be aware of both them and their feelings in addition to ourselves and our own feelings in order to work collaborately and productively. If we lack these core human skills, then we won't want to be somebody that others also want to work with. You can be technically brilliant but if you're lacking in empathy and self-awareness, then you won't work well with others and won't be good at achieving outcomes overall.

What has surprised you the most about the core learning?

As mentioned above, I was quite strongly surprised at my reluctance to write blogs and reflections as part of our core work, even though this kind of writing should be easy for me given my professional experience. I was particularly surprised that I am still overcoming this reluctance.

What were the most challenging aspects of the core learning?

My main challenge was to motivate myself to start writing about my blog and reflect on my experiences, mainly. If I'm being honest with myself, the reflections felt like a chore and I had to force myself to begin each time. Don't get me wrong - this doesn't mean that I think I don't get any benefit from the reflections or blogs, but I still found this work significantly harder than the technical work. Learning about the concepts was fun, and I enjoyed putting them into practice (e.g. regular meditation), but the writing specifically difficult for me to face.

Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on core learning in a web development Bootcamp course?

I think a significant amount of time is focused on core skills because these are the most important skills in our entire careers. While technical ability is obviously important, companies will preferentially hire people with good core human skills over technical skill. Core skills are therefore very important in the tech industry, even if they don't relate to coding per se.

Does the time you spent studying core learning here feel like a waste of time? Should you have just used that time to practise programming instead? Justify your answer.

I don't think this has been a waste of time because as above, the skills we've been developing are very important over the course of our careers. However, I thinking practicing and truly developing these skills is very different to practicing coding. I feel as though the core work that we've done to date is more of an introduction to make sure that we are aware of conceptual skills and have the tools needed to study those skills outselves. Learning those skills with the provided tools is largely up to us and is to be done in our own time, over a long timeframe. Realsitically speaking, we are not going to revolutionise our outlook as people or how we treat others after writing a 30 minute blog post - but perhaps we can sow the seeds that, with sufficient nurturing over a longer time frame, will ultimately help us in workplace and in life in general.
